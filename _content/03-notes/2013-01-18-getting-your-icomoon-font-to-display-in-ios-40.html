Looking at the plethora of icons on my site, you will probably have guessed that I am a huge fan of web font icons. They can really lift a site's design and can be useful for user experience by marking links that leave a site or that download a file, for example.

As long as they are enhancers of content rather than essential parts of it, they are a nice progressive touch which will not affect the overall browsing experience for users on older and less capable browsers.

IcoMoon magic

I used the fantastic IcoMoon App with the Entypo and IcoMoon sets to generate a custom font for this site. Downloading and using the font was a breeze, but during my tests in BrowserStack I discovered that the icons weren't rendering correctly in iOS 4.0. Instead I got those lovely replacements:

Whilst this certainly makes a 'nice' change to the dreaded square boxes, they didn't really fit into my design either.

Bulletproof @Font-Face Syntax to the rescue!

UPDATE

It turns out that the above @font-face syntax is based on the "Base64 Encode & Embed Fonts in CSS" setting in the preferences of the app, which for some reason I had ticked. Now all that code makes perfect sense; it is embedding the font directly. Un-checking this option downloads the CSS with the perfect @font-face syntax, so no reason to follow the manual steps below at all. 

Cheeky little checkbox that.

So I referred back to Fontspring's Bulletproof @Font-Face Syntax:

<strike>@font-face {
  font-family: 'Icons';
  src:  url('fonts/icons-katrinkerber.eot');
  src:  url('fonts/icons-katrinkerber.eot?#iefix') format('embedded-opentype'),
        url('fonts/icons-katrinkerber.woff') format('woff'),
        url('icons-katrinkerber.ttf') format('truetype'),
        url('fonts/icons-katrinkerber.svg#icons-katrinkerber') format('svg');
  font-weight: normal;
  font-style: normal;
}
</strike>

It lived up to its name, as the icons displayed on iOS 4.0 are now the right ones. Yay!

The unhelpful part

Whilst coming across the problem I *seem* to remember that it was caused by the SVG not rendering correctly, but I cannot locate that article again. Looking again today, there seems to be a suggestion floating around that the culprit could be the "Private Use Area" encoding, which we want oh-so-very much to make double-sure our fellow users with screen-readers aren't being pestered.

To my disgrace; I am not sure where exactly the issue lies with IcoMoon's syntax at the moment - if you know, then please enlighten me, as I am dying to know. Whatever the reasons may be, Fontspring's @Font-Face Syntax is still the way to go. It's bulletproof after all.
